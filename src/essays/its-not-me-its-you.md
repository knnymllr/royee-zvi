America is a surface culture. Gratify us! Heal us! And don’t forget to pay attention to us! We want it fast, cheap, and good or don’t bother!

 

This is a culture sold on the idea that hard processes can somehow be easy, that failure isn’t a feature of life but a bug you can patch, that there must be some work-around, some trick to healing all the very real things that ail us. It seems we are always looking for how to cut a corner: in dating, exercise, diet, art, education, and especially mental health. Moreover, there’s this pervasive feeling that if we aren’t celebrated and validated by others for every small win––particularly through social media––it’s as though these things never happened or somehow lose their significance in our lives because *other* people haven’t participated in our “journey”. If it’s *our* journey, why should we bother with what someone else thinks, never mind some random person on the internet?

 

This is the cost of a low-culture that never bothers to dig below the surface and encourages us not to: pop psychology has run rampant with hucksters, grifters, and unregulated everypeople promising “good vibes”, “self-care”, and “spiritual awakenings” of every stripe. “Life coaches” that sell dubious claims of helping you for top dollar and call it “healing”. There are no shortage of trauma educators, spiritual healers, energy workers, guides, yogis, gurus, activists, witches, reiki practitioners, tarot readers, mystics and––of course––those ever-present empaths who are all ready to sell you something at the end of their long-winded Instagram posts––whether it’s an aspirational identity or a $4,000 spiritual retreat to Costa Rica or Mexico or Nicaragua.

 

You might rightly ask: “Why these far away, tropical places and not the comfort of my own home?” Because these people make their living from selling you this idea of a promised land, a utopia, a paradise––that healing can only be done by giving them your life savings and flying to this place, rather than something you can do for free from your room in Des Moines, Detroit, or Denver.

 

High immediacy and low barriers is what’s especially attractive about Insta-therapy––the name coined for the collective online spaces where pseudo-intellectuals barrage you with nonsensical, bloviated lingo that ticks the buzzword boxes of the zeitgeist with words and phrases like “trauma”, “toxic”, “shadow work”, “gaslighting”, “problematic”, and “inner child” that serve to either confuse the average person, or––by denying their sensible confusion––lulls them into going along with the mistaken belief that these people must *be* smart because they *sound* smart. Mash together a confetti-blast of jargon, and suddenly you can amass a cultic, devoted following that hangs onto your every word and is willing to spend big bucks for access to you.

 

There are no shortage of configurations these things can take. There are “spiritual bookkeepers” who don’t just help you with your taxes but promise to: *“help conscious creators and healers gain clarity around their finances by integrating strategy w/spirituality”.* As though there are creators who are unconscious? As though the IRS has a line item for spirituality?

 

Or coaches who claim that they can: *“help you connect to your pelvis to release shame & reclaim authenticity”.* Whose shame? Whose authenticity?



Or this person, who claims that: *“Becoming rich as fuck is one of the biggest acts of resistance you can do. Be your ancestor’s wildest dream.”* I guess capitalism is in now. Ancestors, I’m on my way to riches, just need to manifest it!

 

Or this event which promises to: *“...take you on an epic journey––from the inner workings and shadows of your Inner Autumn and destructive Kali energy force, to the Creatrix manifestation power of your Inner Winter menstrual energy. Then you will rebirth in your Inner Spring with practical tools to implement this wisdom into action, and end in your Inner Summer, where you are ready to radiate, shine and share everything you've learned with the outside world.”*

 

Only a $333 deposit, $999 a ticket for the early bird special (don’t you love it when numerology and capitalism collide?), and a regular ticket price of only $1,227 (I’m sure that doesn’t include the flight + taxi to the jungle). What a bargain­­––if only you could understand exactly what this all meant.

 

Never mind the obvious pattern that emerges when you look at who makes up the majority of these connoisseurs of healing: the same mostly well-off white women that have co-opted nearly every social justice movement. Never mind that the only people who can shell out a thousand bucks for a “nude river dip in the Machuca River” are also typically white and well-off. Never mind that it’s this romanticizing and appropriating of these foreign countries and cultures so you can host your expensive retreat that would ordinarily get these white women cancelled for being colonizers, or something. Apparently you get a pass if you’re providing cacao rituals that you learned growing up in suburban New Jersey. The sheer audacity of the grift alone can stun you––or motivate you to conjure up a grift of your own.

 

And just imagine being one of those people who do shell out the money. If you felt that in all the koombaya of those five days in the jungle you didn’t get your money’s worth, would you say so? If the grift were obvious, would you name it? Or would you recount your experiences breathlessly to your friends and family back home? After all, no one wants to admit they were swindled.

 

This bad language seems to infect nearly every part of our lives. It’s no different from the depressing corpo-speak we’re subjected to in the workplace: “circle back”, “leverage opportunities”, “human capital”, “executing objectives”, “value-added”, “going forward”, “long-term gains”, “sustainability”, “achieve synergy”, “harness the power”, “monetize deliverables”, “optimize time”, “onboarding”, “incentivize labor”, “marching orders”, etc.

 

Or in poetry, where Instagram has once again played a key role in popularizing and elevating poetry that readers claim “is deep” without actually being deep or intellectually challenging whatsoever. Rupi Kaur might be the most recognized and controversial for her role in mainstreaming what reads less like a finely-tuned poem and more like a scribbled sentence plucked at random from a teenager’s diary––



except <br/>
with line breaks  <br>
added to  <br>
make you feel like this is deep  <br>
and is saying something  <br>
profound


The point is, if you dress it up with enough jazzy words, you too can get people to believe in anything. It’s marketing in its most cynical form. In all of these examples, vagueness is rewarded while clarity is shunned. That is by design. Being vague on purpose shrouds something in mystery and inaccessibility (even if it reads like gobbledygook) luring thoughtful people who need real help into dead-ends of worthless parodies of real solutions. Part of the problem is that social media is great at feeding you pastel-colored carousels that aim to make the private setting of the therapist’s office into viral, bite-sized, wide-net public service announcements geared to make you think: “Oh this totally sounds like what I suffer from” while nursing you down the guru rabbit hole. This has led to an epidemic of self-diagnosis and labeling which can cause real harm.

 

Indeed, what many therapists warn about Insta-therapy is that not only are words and ideas constantly misused and bastardized but that because each person’s struggle is unique to *them*, they wonder how Instagram could possibly be a place of real introspection, guidance, and healing if it only serves to garner followers, increase likes and shares, and ultimately financial gain for the content creator?

 

Therapy speak has also been weaponized to a frightening degree. We all know at least one person who armors themselves with pseudo-therapy language to deflect blame, avoid responsibility, and generally to make it seem like *you’re* the problem, not them (“Oh, that’s just the Gemini in me). This is the kind of person who, rather than having the courage to end a friendship, can somehow weave even the most thinly-veiled disgust into some variation of a text like this: “I’m really trying to honor my boundaries this year and in trying to center my needs to maximize safety, I need to ask for space because interacting with you is really problematic and is a trigger for me, thanks.”

 

Essentially, a niced-up, passive aggressive way of telling you to get lost rather than saying the mean but true thing that might hurt someone’s feelings: “I don’t want to be friends with you anymore”. Never mind the fact that we’ve normalized having important or uncomfortable discussions over text rather than face to face.

 

Like all performative language––from its use in social justice circles to conversations about gender identity or politics––therapy-speak nullifies the power of the words being used, robs them of their definitions, and flattens them to meaninglessness before hurling them into the blob of word salads profile-wide. But that’s not how those wielding them see it. In fact, the same language once crafted into esoteric theses by a small cadre of academic elites who looked upon the unenlightened masses with disdain are suddenly accessible to everyone––even those most prone to misunderstand, misinterpret, and misread it (if there is even something intelligible to be read into).

 

Speaking to *The New Yorker,* author Lori Gottlieb says that the problem with this kind of “armchair therapy” or “Insta-therapy,” is that it can distort a “deeply relational, nuanced, contextual process into something ego-directed, as if the point were always, ‘I’m the most important person and I need to take care of myself.’ ”

 

At the heart of it all is a fundamental issue with not only the way we use language, but how we read, comprehend, and interpret it. We suffer from a lack of context. We skim click-bait headlines. We digest tweets. We are misinformed, underinformed, and overinformed. We want knowledge without having to work for it. We want to heal linearly. And, worst of all, we’ve mistaken accessibility to truth as truth itself.
